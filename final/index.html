A fruit-scented miasma drifts across hallways, hides in lockers and emanates from bathrooms around the country. Teenagers clutch sleek, pastel-hued devices advertising flavors such as cranberry puncher, peach mango watermelon and pineapple strawnana.

In the last 15 years, decades worth of progress from anti-smoking groups in lowering nicotine use have been halted by a spike in youth vaping.

About 2.8 million youth currently use tobacco products, according to a survey by the National Youth Tobacco Survey. While the amount of youth vaping has slightly declined in the last few years after a spike in e-cigarette use in the 2010s, the majority of adolescents are consistently using the same, colorful tobacco device: Elf bars.

VAPING HISTORY

Bennett Kelly, a high school senior in Costa Mesa, California vaping a Puff Bar, one of a number of disposable e-cigarette brands that remain available. Photo by Jenna Schoenefeld for The New York Times.

E-cigarettes were first developed in the early 2000s as an alternative to smoking. The devices contain an atomizers and cartridge with either nicotine or non-nicotine liquid solution in addition to a battery component.

E-cigarette use among U.S. middle school and high school students increased by 900% between 2011-2015, according to the CDC. By 2014, they were the most popular tobacco device used among adolescents.

After a brief decline, the rate of youth smoking doubled between 2017 and 2019, surpassing the increased use of the early 2010s, according to the National Institute of Health. While youth vaping has decreased in recent years, public experts question whether decline signifies a long-term shift in vaping trends, especially since the drop came during the COVID-19 pandemic.

MARKETING

Juul, an e-cigarette company introduced in 2015, made a splashy entrance to the vaping market, using colorful advertising and young models to feature their products.

“These advertisements clearly resonated with a younger demographic, school-age teens, which seek to emulate the cool and trendy look of playful 20-something models,” researchers at the Stanford University Research on the Impact of Tobacco said.

Since Juul, other e-cigarette companies, including Breeze and Elf, have emulated similar campaign tactics.

Photos from the Stanford University Research on the Impact of Tobacco show tobacco ads depicting teenage or education-related themes

WHY ARE ELF BARS SO POPULAR?

Among youth who vape, one in four use their device daily, and 90% use flavored e-cigarettes. More than 50% of youth who reported using e-cigarettes last year said they used Elf bars, according to the National Youth Tobacco Survey.

Adolescents’ affinity for flavored devices partly drives Elf bars’ popularity. A loophole in federal regulations allows flavors to be sold in disposable e-cigarettes, like Elf bars, while flavors in refillable cartridge-based devices like Juuls are banned. If high schoolers want flavored vapes, Elf bars are one of their only options.

Elf bars are also relatively cheap as a one-time purchase. Considering adolescents lack fully-developed decision centers, they may go for a cheaper price tag without factoring in the long-term cost of disposable e-cigarettes.

The ergonomic design of the Elf bar also stands out against some of its bulky competitors. It’s small and easy to conceal, allowing adolescents to hide the device from parents and teachers. The pastel ombre exterior is visually appealing and improved functionality allows consumers to start smoking as soon as they take the device out of the package.

A survey by the National Youth Tobacco Survey found that flavoring is one of the top reasons teenagers gave for using e-cigarettes in 2016. In the seven years since that survey, the percentage of teenagers who use flavored e-cigarettes has climbed.

REGULATIONS

Public health building at the University of Maryland on Dec. 14, 2023. Photo by Olivia Borgula.

A 2018 FDA regulation requires tobacco products to include a nicotine warning label in a large, legible font.

In 2019, the Trump administration moved to ban flavored e-cigarettes in an effort to combat youth vaping, but retreated under pressure from political advisors and lobbyists who warned the ban would cause Trump to lose political support.

The same year, a federal law raised the minimum age to purchase any tobacco products, including e-cigarettes, from 18 to 21.

In 2019, Massachusetts, Michigan, Montana, New York, Oregon, Rhode Island, Utah and Washington issued emergency rules to temporarily ban the sale of flavored e-cigarettes amid a record-breaking surge in youth vaping.

As a result of legal challenges, these orders were blocked in Michigan, New York, Oregon and Utah. The Montana and Washington state orders have expired. Massachusetts, New York and Rhode Island later enacted permanent bans.

California has prohibited the sale of both flavored e-cigarettes and menthol cigarettes since 2022

Despite state-wide bans, online shopping allows teenagers to purchase flavored e-cigarettes because most laws don't define e-commerce businesses as retailers. Following the California ban, online sales of cigarettes and vaping products increased significantly, according to research from UC San Diego

TODAY

Vapor from the University of Maryland's NextGen Energy Program on Dec. 14, 2023. Photo by Olivia Borgula.

According to findings from this year’s National Youth Tobacco Survey, 10% of students currently use any tobacco products. The amount of youth vaping declined from last year, but an increased number of middle schoolers used tobacco products while usage decreased among high schoolers.

E-cigarette use is reported at a higher level among LGBTQ+ and transgender youth as well as those reporting psychological distress, according to the National Institute of Health.

Cigarette and cigar use among youth remains at an all-time low.

WHY DOES THIS MATTER?

Public health building at the University of Maryland on Dec. 14, 2023. Photo by Olivia Borgula.

One bc5000 Elf bar containing 500 puffs is approximately equivalent to the nicotine content of 500 cigarettes, or 25 packs. Nicotine exposure during adolescence can harm the developing brain and can hinder learning, memory and attention, according to the CDC.

Despite tighter restrictions and increased scrutiny of e-cigarettes in recent years, there is not significant legislation or regulation to prevent youth vaping, according to Stanford researchers.

A 2018 National Institute on Drug Abuse survey found that most teenagers believe their e-cigarettes only contain flavoring and more than 10% didn't know what was in their device, but a 2015 CDC study found that 99% of e-cigarettes sold in assessed venues in the U.S. contained nicotine.

According to the CDC:

  • Almost 3,000 people were hospitalized during 2019 and early 2020 with severe lung damage from vaping
  • Symptoms typically start gradually, with shortness of breath or chest pain before more severe breathing difficulty and eventually hospital admission.
  • Experts suspect a form of vitamin E — called vitamin E acetate — exists in some e-cigarettes containing THC, and could be the cause lung injury associated with vaping. Other contaminants and other factors, such as pre-existing lung disease, may also play a role.
  • The number of new cases has declined dramatically since September 2019, likely due to public health messaging about a link between THC in e-cigarettes and lung diseases, and removal of vitamin E acetate from e-cigarettes.
